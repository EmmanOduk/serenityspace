Hellebore

- The genus Helleborus includes more than 20 species, originating in Europe and asia
- They can reach 12 to 48 inches in height
- Can be divided into two groups: caulescent (have a stem) & acaulescent (don’t have a stem)
- Produces glossy, green leaves which remain on the plant all year long (never acc falls off)
- Hellebore produces individual flowers consisting of 5 sepals & cup shaped nectaries. Wild types of hellebore produce green, greenish purple and golden yellow colored flowers. But new varieties can be created through the hybridization of existing species (white, yellow, pink, red, grey, deep purple and nearly black)
- They bloom during the winter and early spring
- The name originates from Greek works “helle” meaning “to take away” and “bora” meaning “food”. It refers to the emetic (induces vomiting) properties of this plant. Helleborus are potentially fatal.
- In this past, black hellebore was used for cleansing of the body and in treatment for paralysis and mental disorders.
- They are pest resistant, and specifically the White hellebore are highly toxic
- The species H. niger, also called Christmas rose (popular in cottage gardens), contains glycosides which can cause cardiac problems. It was used for its emetic property to rid the body of worms but it didn’t rly work well n caused bare deaths. Can also cause skin irritation
